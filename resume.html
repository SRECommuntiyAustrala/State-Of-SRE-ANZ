// Introduction

SRE in Australia is broader than most engineers realise.

Since recruiting in the Australian infrastructure market across Cloud Engineering, DevOps and Site Reliability Engineering, one thing stands out: SRE in Australia is something a lot of engineers take part in without necessarily being in a dedicated role.

Businesses from startup to enterprise are now implementing reliability practices and seeing the value they can add. Observability is a hot talking point in the current market, with multiple businesses hiring engineers with observability-focused titles. But Site Reliability is much broader than this — while Observability is a core pillar, it's key to look at the full scope of the role.

Not all SRE roles are the same, and following the Google method isn't always practical. The key is having multiple resume versions based on a core template of your skills. It helps hiring managers remove cognitive bias and makes you more relevant to each specific role.

// Structure

The big thing most resumes miss.

It's easy to write something like "automated infrastructure build using Terraform." But what does that actually tell a hiring manager about you? The answer is: not much. Good structure is what separates resumes that get interviews from ones that don't.

For every responsibility or project on your resume, think about three things:

W

Why

What was the problem space? What were you solving and why did it matter?

H

How

What action did you take? What tools, methods or decisions were involved?

I

Impact

What was the outcome? What changed for your customers, engineers, or the business?

This template gives hiring managers a genuine insight into the role you played, the projects and technology you worked with, and the outcome it had — whether that's on end-users of a product or the engineers you're working alongside.

// The STAR method

Apply STAR thinking to everything you write.

The Why / How / Impact structure maps directly to the STAR method used in interviews — Situation, Task, Action, Result. If you write your resume bullets this way, you're already halfway prepared for behavioural interview questions.

Bad: "Automated infrastructure build using Terraform."

Better: "Our manual provisioning process was causing 3–4 hour delays on new service deployments. Designed and implemented a Terraform module library across three AWS accounts, reducing provisioning time to under 10 minutes and eliminating a category of configuration drift incidents."

The second version tells the hiring manager what the problem was, what you did about it, and what changed as a result. That's the difference between being remembered and being filtered out.

// SRE principles

Map your experience to the core SRE principles.

Start thinking about how your current and previous work maps to the foundational pillars of SRE. You don't need all of them — but showing depth in even two or three signals genuine SRE thinking rather than just infrastructure work with a different title.

Embracing risk SLOs Eliminating toil Monitoring Automation Release engineering Simplicity

For each principle, ask yourself: have I done something in my current role that relates to this? Even if your title isn't SRE, the work often is — it just needs to be framed correctly.

// Example: monitoring

How to write about improving reliability.

A good framework when writing about monitoring, observability or reliability improvements is to think about the chain of change you drove:

Monitoring improvement formula
Need a new metric which is X,
this will indicate Y,
which enables driving action Z.

This framework — monitor, investigate, alert, propose uplift — shows you're thinking about observability as a system that drives action, not just a collection of dashboards. Hiring managers and SRE leaders respond strongly to candidates who can articulate this chain clearly.

// Incidents

The thing nobody puts on their resume, but should.

// Underused signal

Mentioning incidents, service failures and how you handled incident response almost never shows up on SRE resumes. It should.

It's great that the services you look after have an uptime of 99.99% — but what about when they go down? For a hiring manager focused on SRE, this is one of the most powerful talking points in both a resume and an interview. It demonstrates a range of characteristics that SREs need in their day-to-day: technical depth, mental resilience, and cultural alignment with blameless postmortem practices.

Think about incidents you've been involved in. How did you detect it? What was your role in the response? How did the team learn from it? What changed afterwards? That story — told with structure — is more valuable than a list of tools.

Example: "Led incident response for a P1 outage affecting the payments service. Coordinated cross-team communication, drove root cause analysis and authored a postmortem that resulted in three reliability improvements being prioritised in the next quarter. MTTR reduced from 47 minutes to 12 minutes over the following six months."

That kind of bullet tells a hiring manager more about you as an SRE than any certification or tool list ever could.
